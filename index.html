Professional Persona Arc

From Self-Perception
to Reputation

Professional Persona Video
01

Foundation

Hockey taught me how to win. More importantly it taught me that winning alone means nothing if the team loses. I carry that into every room I walk into. People notice. The feedback is always the same. Reliable, high energy, shows up.

02

Communication

My Self-Perception Inventory revealed that I'm wired to be direct and I move fast. What I learned is that moving fast without bringing people along is just noise. I've built a real habit around listening and it changed how rooms respond to me.

03

Growth

Internships, feedback sessions, tough conversations. I don't treat any of it as a verdict on who I am. I treat it as data. Openness to learning is the only advantage you can compound forever. I've leaned into that fully.

04

Reputation

People describe me as easy to work with and they trust my instincts. In the finance world you get written off fast if you're only one of those. I've put real work into being both and it's showing up in how people talk about me.

05

What I'm Fixing

Honest version. My enthusiasm can outrun my structure. I intend to energize a room and I do. But sometimes it reads as scattered. I've been working on channeling that into something more deliberate. The goal isn't less energy. It's better aim.

06

The Goal

I'm entering finance knowing exactly who I am. The gap between how I see myself and how others see me is small and it's closing. I want to build something where being real and being rigorous aren't in tension. That's the whole goal.

The Alignment Question

I came into this process expecting gaps. What I found instead was a mirror that mostly agreed with me, with a few sharp edges worth paying attention to.

The words I'd use for myself: driven, sociable, analytical, enthusiastic. Turns out, so would the people around me. Survey respondents and teammates reflected almost the same picture back. That's not something you can fake. It means the version of me I think I'm putting out is actually landing.

But here's where it gets interesting. The one place things diverged wasn't in how I'm perceived, it was in pace. I operate fast. I make the call, move to the next thing, and assume everyone's still with me. What the feedback told me is that in high-stakes environments, thoroughness isn't a slowdown, it's the signal. A finance room doesn't reward whoever gets there first. It rewards whoever got there right.

So the adjustment isn't a personality overhaul. It's a sequencing shift: structure first, then enthusiasm. Let the model speak before I do. I've always had the instincts, now I'm building the discipline to match. That combination is what separates someone who moves fast from someone who builds something that lasts.
